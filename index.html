Bright Minds Web Application

Bright Minds Learning Academy is a private tutoring company whose owner asked me to design a modern website. The final product was a website application that uses a Cascading Style Sheet and HTML front end with a JavaScript back end. Additionally, the application uses a Firebase database to store and track email subscriptions, as seen in the purple “Newsletter” page. When signed in as the owner using Google Authentication, the owner can track grades, progress reports, and blog posts.

One of the challenges I experienced while gathering product requirements was that the client wasn’t a native English speaker and struggled to explain some of the features that she desired. Thus, to understand my client’s needs, I prepared different front-end prototypes on a white board, where the client would be able to choose the design that she desired, even suggesting her own by drawing them on the board. Overall, being forced to gather product requirements in a partially nonverbal setting has improved my overall communication skills, a quality that’s especially important in Product Management.

Another challenge I faced was having to serve as multiple roles in a Software Development team in quick succession. For example, one moment, I was writing a function that retrieves information from the database and the next moment, I was writing unit tests to test that function. Overall, since this was my first major outside-of-the-classroom project, the experience has cemented my foundation as a Software Engineer.

Slot Machine

For my Mechatronics class, my partner and I designed and implemented a standard slot machine using two PIC18F4520 microcontrollers (each used to handle logical and mechanical systems), four Arduino H-Bridge motor drivers, two sets of IR sensors, and four stepper motors. The slot machine’s interface involves a single LED button next to a small slot that senses coin input. All outputs are projected onto three 3D-Printed slot wheels that spun in random locations. The project was one of the first electronic appliances I made: a foot-tall classic slot machine that took coin inputs and outputted winnings depending on the given reel.

Coming from a computer science background, I had almost no hardware experience. Overcoming this challenge took hours of time spent in the laboratory learning electrical concepts, reading data sheets, and testing circuits on a trial and error basis. During this process, I utilized whatever resources I could find, including TA/Professor office hours, my roommate’s DC circuits textbook, and YouTube videos. Not only has the experience has given me designing, building, and testing experience with imbedded systems, but it has also improved my ability to self-learn, which involves asking good questions and utilizing learning tools, a skill that is viable in industry.

Overall, the Slot Machine project has encouraged me to explore and interact with areas outside of my comfort zone.

Food Compass

For the Food Compass Challenge at the Indy Civic Challenge 2018, I worked with a team of three and developed a static web application using HTML and CSS for the front end, JavaScript for the back end, and Java for data scrubbing. The goal of the project was to provide a pertinent solution to the food insecurity problem that afflicts Marion County and the city of Indianapolis. The web application presents the user with a map of Marion County.

The map is focused on Indianapolis, and features pins that correlate to various local and state-based agencies. These points are linked to various local, charities, churches, and established programs’ websites that can provide resources and assistance for those with food insecurity. The checkbox can filter irrelevant, open and accessible agencies to the user, while the pins themselves on the map provide a myriad of information, such as the name of the agency, its address, phone number, website URL, distance, and relevant information regarding procedures and requirements.

Since this was my team’s first hackathon, some of us had initial feelings of self-doubt: that our lack of programming experience leaves us handicapped in creative potential. Although we were initially self-doubtful, we developed into a strongly functioning team where each member could incorporate their strengths in ways to finalize a usable product. This experience has taught me to find ways to utilize my unique skillset to have a large impact on the project. Additionally, the experience has strengthened my ability to work in a team, a skill that is pertinent to building relationships with coworkers.

Optimistically, my team and I believed that we produced a product that may provide a viable way to aid the ailment of food insecurity in Indianapolis. At worst, we acquired a lot of experience, made some new friends, and seized the opportunity to show support for the Indianapolis community at large.
